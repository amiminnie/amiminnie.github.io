Notes from the Dry Zone

17 apr 2026

Travelling far away to see your family is weird, some people love it, some people don't. While I totally love the travelling part, it also means I have to boymode the entire time. With all this gender dysphoria, I have learned to find the little happy things in life in a sea of testosterone, this article documents a totally normal family trip, but with the little details that make it beautiful.

Half asleep

The day started off pretty hectic, waking up at around 3:30, we were kind of worried that we might miss the train. We were supposed to leave the house by 4:30 as our first train would arrive at 5. I was still half-asleep understandably, pretty much everyone else too. While we did pack our bags the night before, we still had a lot to prepare. For example, we totally underestimated how long it would take to make some sandwitches. At the end though, we did somehow manage to arrive at the station, with time to spare.

Waiting at an empty train station with the salty breeze from the Indian Ocean, and to be fair, the smell of cigarettes and the rave of music from the local crackheads, it was definetly a vibe. Our mighty Japanese built, class S8 multiple unit finally neared the platform just a few minutes behind schedule, and the beaming headlights highlighted the smoke from the said people, revealing their hidden corners.

While a part of me was expecting a packed train full of school students and office workers, it was just as empty as... my imagination. This one, is just a commuter train that could take us to the main railway hub of the country, Colombo Fort, where you can pretty much take a train to anywhere on the island. While it's usually extremely busy at this time of the day, it was a holiday, Sinhala and Tamil New Years day!

Even with the creeks and rumble of this grandma of a train set, it felt peaceful, as the normal hustle and bustle of the morning rush hour had stopped for a day. The Coastal Line, the route we are taking right now, runs along the south west coast of the island, and the occasional spray from the ocean waves crashing onto the stone barrier, the very thing responsible for the rough shape of this train, was bringing some nice atmosphere to the scene.

Some children were just sleeping laying down on those bench style commuter seats, and there was this guy who moved his entire little bicycle shopt into the carriege. He was balancing his bicycle on a beans that standing passengers would hold onto, and was fixing it with the tools he would pull out of the freezer box sized toolbox. This mechanic would drop his tools, or the little nuts and bolts multiple times, as the train rocked from side to side. most likely, the toolbox was indeed just a retired ice box used by fishermen, who would also use this train to transport their harvest to the city.

We finally arrived at colombo fort at around 5:30, with plenty of time to spare for the departure of the main train of the day, at 6:00. Expecting a sleek modern Indian built Class S13 multiple unit, we were disappointed to see a class M6 with Astra rail carrieges refurbished by Tantri, nutorios for a giving a bad experience. I'm not saying the M6 is bad in any way, it definetly had its prime days running the nostalgic Udarata Manike (Upcountry Maiden) back then, but we have way better options nowadays when it comes to efficiency and experience.

You'd expect 2nd Class to be at least decent, since these coaches don't come in a 1st Class varient anyways so this is the best it gets. Every single window was barely locking in the open position, the margins were always too large for the tiny nibs holding them up would just slip away with the slightest motion. It felt like wack-a-mole, but we were the moles when the windows started falling one by one.
Honestly, this wouldn't be a blog if I were to list out every problem here, instead be a complaint file.

Making this whole situation pettier was a Class S13A idling just a few platforms away, a varient of the normal Class S13, but with 10 air conditioned reserved first class carrieges, what we'd consider luxury my Sri Lanka Railways standards. It was the Kankasanthurai Express, a luxury train only on the holidays.

Let's chill out a bit, the station was dead silent, except for the occasional announcement. The station was large, but not a person in sight as far as the eye can see. The sky started getting brighter, from blue, to pink, to yellow, it was a perfect gradient. With small clouds scattered around and the crescent moon, I could definetly see that being a painting.
